Web-accessibility Project: Revamping the de Young Museum Site

DH150, SUMMER 2021 - PROJECT DOCUMENTATION BY VALENTIN NGUYEN

INTRODUCTION

Web accessibility is an important factor when designing and developing websites so that anyone, especially those with disabilities, can use them. However, many sites have accessibility barriers that make them difficult for some users to use and interact with.

This project is to test and improve the web-accessibility of websites that display information about museums. Over a couple months during the summer of 2021, I researched museum webpages and created a mockup of the de Young Museums homepage for the Digital Humanities 150: Web-coding course at UCLA.

RESEARCH

Below are websites of five museums located in San Francisco, California. As a San Francisco native, I found these museums interesting to investigate because I have personally gone to most of these museums in person and enjoyed my time there. However, I would like to see if their websites are as easy to navigate as their exhibitions. To start my research, I searched for these websites on the web browser Google Chrome and used a Chrome Development tool called Lighthouse to diagnose the accessibility and search engine optimization (SEO) rankings of those websites.

Based on my research, I have found that most of the websites I have audited lack accessible names and labels for buttons or interactive links, as well as poor color contrast ratios between background and foreground colors. Their accessiblity scores were especially low throughout all of them, ranging between scores of 75-85 on average. Each of their SEO scores were fairly OK, but could still be improved by adding metadata and tags. These accessibility issues could be fixed by adding and creating clearer labels to buttons and links, both internally in the code and the design. I was surprised to find these issues because I personally found most of these websites to be legible and fairly easy to navigate. However, I understand that users that use assistive technology and screen readers or have disabilities may have a more difficult time navigating websites that lack proper accessibility features.

DESIGN PROCESS

For this project, I have decided to investigate and create a mockup for the de Young Museum website, which got an accessibility score of 74 and an SEO score of 75. A few accessibility issues on the de Young Museum homepage include not using ARIA labels (which could help better the experience for users that use assistive technology, like screen readers), illegibility of content from the colors not having that great of a color contrast ratio, and links not having discernible names and labels. The de Young Museum's SEO ranking could be better by formatting the content in a way for users to more easily understand the app's content, such as adding descriptive text to links. Also, the links on the website are current crawlable and could be formatted for crawlers to better understand the content.

Below is my process in developing and testing a mockup version of the de Young Museum website to improve its accessibility and SEO scores.

SEMANTIC STRUCTURE: To ensure the semantic HTML of the website, I restructured the content to make the page more semantically structured. I also added bypass blocks, such as 'Skip to content' and 'Back to top' buttons.



COLOR CONTRAST: To style the structured HTML, I applied CSS codes to check the color-contrast of the background and text colors through the process of making design variations in order to enture the compliance with perceptual level of accessibility guidelines. In this process, I applied CSS codes to change the typography and hyperlink colors, as well as the background colors of different semantic structures to create dark mode, monochromatic, and light mode variations of the webpage.



KEYBOARD ACCESSIBILITY: To improve keyboard accessibility and tab-order, I added tab index and interactive items. I making sure every element on the webpage is accessible and operable, add hyperlinks and buttons to make more elements interactive, made sure the focus is visible and that the buttons were legible enough so that users with screen readers can distinguish the buttons from on another, and ensured that the overall order feels natural in reading.



SCREEN-READER / ARIA-LABEL: To support assistive technologies (screen readers), I added HTML's label elements and ARIA- attributes. These can add more information to elements that may not be visible to the eye, but are helpful for users that interpret the content through other means such as screen-readers. I added buttons to switch between light mode and dark mode.



REFLECTION

In conclusion, I have learned a lot while taking the DH150 course in regards to improving web accessibility and SEO with web-coding. Before, I thought that the original website of the museum of my choosing, the de Young Museum, was already accessible and easy to navigate enough. Little did I know, the webpage was not the most accessible it could be with less than ideal accessibility and SEO scores (accessibility score = 75, SEO score = 75). I thought that the design of webpage was already well done, but I didn't know how inaccessible the functioning of the webpage was for users that user assistive technology, like screen readers. Now, after creating a mockup of the website by making it more semantically structured, checking and editing the color contrast, improving keyboard accessibility and tab-order, and allowing it to support assistive technologes, I was able to increase the website's accessibility and SEO scores to 100 for both! In the future, I will definitely use the knowledge I learned about accessibility in my later works, especially projects that involve users.
